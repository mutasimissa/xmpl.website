# Value Proposition Framework

## Structure

A complete value proposition has seven elements:

1. **Audience** -- who this is for
2. **Problem** -- the pain or gap they experience
3. **Desired outcome** -- what success looks like for them
4. **Promise** -- what you deliver
5. **Differentiation** -- why you over alternatives
6. **Proof** -- evidence the promise is real
7. **Urgency** -- reason to act now rather than later

## Rule

The value proposition must be concrete enough to shape:

- headline
- subheadline
- CTA
- benefit blocks
- proof sections

## How to derive each element

### Audience

Identify the specific role, industry, and context of the buyer. Avoid generic
descriptions like "businesses" or "professionals." Pull from
`business/02-brand-strategy.md` personas and `business/04-business-model.md`
target segments.

**Questions to ask:**

- Who signs off on this purchase or decision?
- What is their job title and daily responsibility?
- What industry or vertical are they in?
- What size is their organization?

### Problem

Define the concrete pain the buyer faces before finding the solution. The
problem should be specific enough that the buyer recognizes it immediately. Pull
from persona pain points in `business/02-brand-strategy.md`.

**Questions to ask:**

- What is the buyer struggling with right now?
- What has failed or underperformed for them?
- What is the cost of inaction (time, money, risk)?
- How do they describe this problem in their own words?

### Desired outcome

Describe the end state the buyer wants. This is not the product -- it is the
result the buyer gets after using the product. Frame it in the buyer's language.

**Questions to ask:**

- What does the buyer's day look like after the problem is solved?
- What metric improves?
- What risk goes away?
- What capability do they gain?

### Promise

State what the business delivers. The promise bridges the problem and the
desired outcome. It should be a single, clear statement.

**Questions to ask:**

- What do you actually deliver (service, product, platform)?
- What result can you commit to?
- How would you complete the sentence: "We help [audience] achieve [outcome] by..."?

### Differentiation

Explain why this solution is better than alternatives. Alternatives include
competitors, in-house efforts, and doing nothing. Pull from
`business/02-brand-strategy.md` competitive positioning.

**Questions to ask:**

- What do competitors fail to deliver?
- What unique capability, method, or experience does this business have?
- What would a buyer lose by choosing an alternative?
- Is there a structural advantage (speed, depth, geography, specialization)?

### Proof

List evidence that the promise is real. Proof reduces perceived risk. Types of
proof: credentials, certifications, case results, client references, process
documentation, team experience, data points.

**Questions to ask:**

- What results have you delivered before?
- What credentials or certifications do you hold?
- Can you describe your process in enough detail to build confidence?
- What third-party validation exists (awards, partnerships, audits)?

**Connection to business files:** Pull proof from `business/02-brand-strategy.md`
(proof elements section), `business/04-business-model.md` (delivery method), and
any case studies or testimonials referenced in business intake.

### Urgency

Give the buyer a reason to act now. Urgency can be situational (market shift,
regulatory deadline), capacity-based (limited availability), or
consequence-based (cost of delay).

**Questions to ask:**

- Is there a market or regulatory change creating time pressure?
- Does the business have limited capacity or intake windows?
- What is the measurable cost of waiting another quarter?
- Is there a seasonal or cyclical driver?

## Strong vs weak value propositions

### Weak

> "We provide innovative solutions for modern businesses looking to improve
> their operations."

Problems: generic audience, no specific problem, no proof, "innovative" is
unsupported, no urgency.

### Strong

> "Operations leaders in industrial plants lose 12+ hours per week to manual
> monitoring. Our visibility platform consolidates sensor data into a single
> dashboard so you can detect issues before they cause downtime -- backed by
> 15 years of field deployment across GCC facilities."

Strengths: named audience, quantified problem, clear outcome, specific proof,
implicit urgency (downtime cost).

### Weak

> "The best platform for your business needs."

Problems: no audience, no problem, no proof, superlative without evidence.

### Strong

> "Procurement teams in mid-size manufacturers spend 3 weeks per quarter
> reconciling supplier data. Our automated intake system cuts that to 2 days
> with zero manual data entry -- used by 40+ manufacturing firms across KSA."

## How the value proposition shapes the homepage

| Value prop element | Homepage section          |
| ------------------ | ------------------------- |
| Promise            | Hero headline             |
| Desired outcome    | Hero subheadline          |
| Problem            | Problem/pain section      |
| Differentiation    | Why-us or comparison      |
| Proof              | Social proof / credentials |
| Urgency            | CTA supporting text       |
| Audience           | Implicit in all copy      |

Each element should appear at least once on the homepage. The hero carries
the heaviest load (promise + outcome), while body sections expand on
differentiation and proof.

## Jobs-to-be-Done framework

Beyond the seven-element structure, the value proposition should decompose the
buyer's world using the JTBD framework:

### Functional jobs

What the buyer is trying to get done -- specific tasks, not abstract goals.
Good: "Launch a website before Q2 fundraise." Bad: "Improve online presence."

### Social jobs

How the buyer wants to be perceived. These drive visual quality expectations
and credibility requirements: "Look like a funded startup" drives different
design than "Look like an established enterprise."

### Emotional jobs

What feelings the buyer seeks (confidence, relief, control) or avoids
(embarrassment, anxiety, regret). Emotional jobs inform CTA language and
pain-section intensity.

## Pain/gain mapping

Every pain in the customer profile must map to a specific pain reliever in the
value map. Every gain must map to a gain creator. This one-to-one mapping
ensures the value proposition is complete -- no claim exists without a
corresponding customer need, and no need exists without a response.

### How to map

1. List all pains from the customer profile
2. For each pain, identify which product/service feature addresses it
3. Describe HOW it addresses it (mechanism, not just "we solve this")
4. Repeat for gains

Unmapped pains reveal product gaps. Unmapped features reveal positioning
opportunities or bloat.

## Proof matrix

Every claim on the website must trace to a row in the proof matrix. The matrix
has four columns:

1. **Claim** -- what the site says (e.g., "Fast delivery")
2. **Evidence** -- what supports it (e.g., "Average 14-day launch time")
3. **Source** -- where the evidence comes from (internal data, case study, etc.)
4. **Status** -- Verified, Assumption, or Gap

Rules:
- Claims with "Verified" status can appear as-is
- Claims with "Assumption" status must be softened ("typically" instead of "always")
- Claims with "Gap" status must either find evidence or be removed
- No superlatives ("best", "fastest", "#1") without verified evidence

## Per-segment positioning

If multiple customer segments exist, write a fit statement for EACH segment.
Different segments have different jobs, pains, and gains -- a single value
proposition cannot serve all equally.

The fit statement follows the pattern:
> For [segment] who [job], [business] is the [category] that [key benefit]
> unlike [alternatives] because [proof].

This per-segment approach feeds directly into:
- Audience-specific landing pages (/for/[segment])
- Segment-specific hero variants
- Targeted CTA language per audience
